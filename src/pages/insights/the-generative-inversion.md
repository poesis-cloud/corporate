Classical systems thinking is **descriptive**. You observe a system, then build a model to understand it: a diagram, an architecture document, an enterprise-architecture repository. The model trails the reality it describes.

That leaves a gap. The description and the running system are two separate artifacts, maintained by different people with different incentives — so they drift. By the time the model is "accurate", the system has moved on. Governance becomes an audit of the past, not a control on the present.

## Inverting the order

GSM makes the **definition primary and generative**. Instead of describing a system after it exists, you *define* it — and the definition is what generates and governs the running thing. Directives and Norms govern flat Structures; the definition carries its own lifecycle; constraints don't merely annotate the system, they *constitute* it.

This is the generative inversion: **constraining becomes defining**. A Norm isn't a note attached to a component — it is part of what the component *is*. Close the loop — definition generates build, build runs, telemetry observes, governance reconciles definition against reality — and the system becomes self-correcting rather than merely documented.

## Quality as viability

Once definitions are generative, quality stops being a checklist applied afterward and becomes **viability**: the capacity of the system to keep satisfying its own governing definitions as its environment changes. Governance is no longer a periodic review; it is a continuous, closed loop.

This is the thesis behind SIE and ITIP — and the subject of the forthcoming foundational paper, *The Generative Inversion: from descriptive to definitional systemics*.
